PALETTE INFO

There are a lot of palettes here now. The downloads for this
group of PixVoxel Wargame sprites are categorized into 24
groups. 8 corresponding to different main paint colors with
sub-folders containing different hair and skin color
combinations that are normal enough for humans; some palettes
are meant to allow showing people who dye their hair, or to
allow for for the possibility of anime conventions allowing
green-haired humans to be a normal, commonplace thing. Another
8 downloads are categorized into the same main paint colors but
use unnatural colors for fire and have "Alien" skin colors
(ranging from milky white with no mouth (palettes 208-215) to
pitch-black with no discernable features of any kind (palettes
224-231), and from deep purple with piercing white eyes
(palettes 280-287) to sickly yellow-green (palettes 248-255)).
There's then a third group of "Zombie" palettes (palettes
296-303), but these aren't just a palette swap, and actually
alter the image to add "battle damage" to the bodies of humans
and vehicles, and swap the head to have a gaping head wound and
a groaning open mouth. The zombies have drab green skin and
lurid green "blood", meant to fill the standard usage of zombies
in many games (especially when localized for sale in Germany) to
indicate they are clearly not human and avoid severe "violent
content" ratings. You can use altered palettes to change blood
to another color (such as red or black) using the same techniques
as changing any other color; the rest of the document goes into
more detail.

Palette numbers that are exact multiples of 8 (including 0) use
"Dark" paint colors with dark red highlights, and are in the
"Dark" subfolder. Palettes that are 1 higher than an exact
multiple of 8 (such as 1, 9, and 17) use "White" paint colors
and are in the "White" subfolder (these do not default to
aliens with pale green skin as in earlier versions; those are
available almost without change at palette 257). After that are
Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, and Purple, then it repeats
to Dark, White, Red, etc. as the main paint color but with a
different skin/hair color combination.

You may want to use an alternate set of palettes for your game,
to add an additional color scheme to the existing many, or to
change an existing one, i.e. you could make all of the
dark-painted units produce purple fire instead of normal colors.
The images in standing_frames and animation_frames are
indexed-palette PNG files. They store a palette once, and then
each pixel is represented by a single index between 0 and 255
that refers to a color in the palette. In various image editors,
it is possible to copy the palette of one indexed image into the
palette of another. That's why the palettes directory has the
304+ existing palettes I've made so far here: so you can edit a
palette and possibly use it to recolor existing images. It may
be easier for some games to do this with a shader that reads,
say, the red channel from an image and uses that as an index
into a palette to find the real color. The blank_palette
download is meant for exactly that. Red 0 (or blue 0, or green
0) in those images corresponds to index 0 (transparent), red
149 corresponds to a color used for mud and grime, etc.

Because there are so many palettes (as mentioned earlier, 320),
it may be difficult for a game to store every single variant.
In fact, for most images of a particular unit with no visible
human, in a particular frame, of a particular action (facing the
same direction), only some palettes will have any difference at
all (typically, "Alien" palettes are different in most cases from
"normal" palettes). A Plane or Plane_P has no visible pilot, so
the palette variants that have different skin or hair color for
humanoids will appear identical if the main paint scheme is the 
same. A Plane_S has visible flames coming out of the back of its
engines, and since "Alien" units have unexplained technology
(and because it looks cool), flames are unnatural colors for
most alien palettes (208 to 295). This means a Plane_S with the
same main paint color will appear identical for all "normal
human" palettes, but for palettes 208 to 295, each variant will
probably look different. The "Zombie" images have a different
image to show the ragged wear and tear on vehicles and clothing
that the walking dead should have, so while you can palette-swap
a normal image to use a Zombie palette, it will look more fitting
with an actual image change. It is possible that a program that
packs many images into a texture atlas (similar in concept to a
spritesheet) may be able to eliminate duplicate images and use
only one image for all copies; the TexturePacker program in
libGDX can do this, for example.

A better approach, that does not require hundreds of large textures
filled with the approximately 3.7 million images here, is to use
only the blank_palette download and palette-swap each image (or
region of a texture atlas) with a shader when you render it, using
a shader uniform to choose the palette, and binding one extra
texture that contains all the 1-pixel-tall palettes you use in
rows that correspond to the numbers for the palettes (which could
be the same as the ones used here). In fact, using shaders is the
recommended approach by the authors of OpenGL to replace older
palette-swapping techniques that relied on hardware support for
indexed color.

Now, here's the rough meanings of what editing a pixel
in the palettes will change.

The color names here are in reverse order from how they
appear in the palette, and there are 4 levels of shading
for most colors. To get the correct location in the palette,
take 253, subtract 4 times the number given (such as 9 for
skin color), and that gives you the x-position of the pixel
with the lightest shading for that color. To its left, there
should be the 3 darker shadings of that same color, and then
it switches to another color.

There is also a palette provided for all of the
terrain, and the numbers mean something completely different
in that. Thankfully, it is organized into groups of 16, which
are visually obvious in the image, and changing, say, all 16
bright-yellow pixels at the left of the image will affect any
occurences of originally-bright-yellow colors, such as some
marks on road terrain, some small dots in jungle terrain, and
probably some others). Some of the heavily shaded colors in
the 16 may be hard to distinguish from their neighbors, but
since they're always in groups of four with the lightest at
the right, it should be easy to figure out what color a change
will affect.
